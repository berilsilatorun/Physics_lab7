# 2. String Harmonics (Guitar String)

**Problem Setup:**
A guitar string is fixed at both ends, creating standing waves.
* Length of the string: $L = 64\text{ cm} = 0.64\text{ m}$
* Fundamental frequency ($f_1$): $330\text{ Hz}$ (One antinode)

**Goal:** Find the speed ($v$) of the wave on this string.

---

### Step 1: Determine the Wavelength ($\lambda$)
For the fundamental frequency (the first harmonic), the string vibrates with a single antinode in the middle and two nodes at the ends. This means the length of the string is exactly **half a wavelength**:

$$L = \frac{\lambda}{2} \implies \lambda = 2L$$

Substitute the length:
$$\lambda = 2 \cdot 0.64$$
$$\lambda = 1.28\text{ m}$$

---

### Step 2: Calculate the Wave Speed ($v$)
Now we use the fundamental wave equation:
$$v = f \cdot \lambda$$

Substitute the frequency ($330\text{ Hz}$) and the wavelength ($1.28\text{ m}$):
$$v = 330 \cdot 1.28$$
$$v = 422.4\text{ m/s}$$

---

**Conclusion:** The speed of the wave on this guitar string is **422.4 m/s**. 

*Technical Note: This speed depends on the tension of the string and its linear mass density ($v = \sqrt{T/\mu}$). By tightening the tuning peg, we increase the tension $T$, which increases $v$ and consequently the frequency $f$, resulting in a higher pitch.*
