## 7. Cyclotron Motion

**Problem:** An electron is accelerated from rest through a potential difference of $V = 5000\text{ V}$. It then enters a region of uniform magnetic field $B = 0.1\text{ T}$, perpendicular to its velocity. What is the radius of the circular path it will follow?

### Constants
Before we start, let's list the fundamental constants for an electron:
* Mass of electron: $m_e \approx 9.11 \times 10^{-31}\text{ kg}$
* Elementary charge: $e \approx 1.60 \times 10^{-19}\text{ C}$

### Step 1: Calculate the Velocity ($v$)
When the electron is accelerated from rest through a potential difference, its electrical potential energy is converted entirely into kinetic energy. 
According to the Law of Conservation of Energy:
$$eV = \frac{1}{2}m_e v^2$$

Solving for the final velocity $v$:
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{2eV}{m_e}}$$

Substituting the known values:
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{2 \cdot (1.60 \times 10^{-19}) \cdot 5000}{9.11 \times 10^{-31}}}$$
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{1.60 \times 10^{-15}}{9.11 \times 10^{-31}}}$$
$$v \approx 4.19 \times 10^7\text{ m/s}$$

### Step 2: Set up the Force Equation for Circular Motion
When the moving electron enters the magnetic field perpendicularly, the Lorentz force acts as a centripetal force, pulling it into a circular path.
$$F_B = F_c$$
$$evB = \frac{m_e v^2}{r}$$

We can simplify this by canceling one $v$ and solving for the radius $r$:
$$r = \frac{m_e v}{eB}$$

### Step 3: Calculate the Radius ($r$)
Now we plug in the velocity we found in Step 1, along with the given magnetic field strength ($B = 0.1\text{ T}$):
$$r = \frac{(9.11 \times 10^{-31}) \cdot (4.19 \times 10^7)}{(1.60 \times 10^{-19}) \cdot (0.1)}$$
$$r = \frac{3.817 \times 10^{-23}}{1.60 \times 10^{-20}}$$
$$r \approx 2.38 \times 10^{-3}\text{ m}$$

**Conclusion:** 
The radius of the circular path followed by the electron will be approximately **$2.38\text{ mm}$** (or $2.38 \times 10^{-3}\text{ meters}$).
